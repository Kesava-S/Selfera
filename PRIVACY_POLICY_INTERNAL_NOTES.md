The ICO provides a free LIA template at ico.org.uk. Each LIA is a 1 to 2 page internal document following the three-part test (purpose, necessity, balancing).

Note 2: Recognised legitimate interests under DUAA
The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 introduced a shortlist of processing activities that automatically qualify as legitimate interests without needing a full balancing test. The relevant ones for Selfera are:
Network and information security
Fraud prevention
These still require documentation but the balancing test is simplified.

Note 3: PECR compliance for marketing emails
All marketing emails to individuals require opt-in consent under PECR unless the soft opt-in exception applies (existing customers only, related services, opt-out on collection, opt-out in every message). Pre-ticked checkboxes are not valid consent. Every signup form must have an unticked opt-in checkbox.

Note 4: Cookie consent must precede GA4 and Meta Pixel
The policy claims analytics and advertising cookies only load after consent. This must be technically enforced by the cookie banner. If GA4 or Meta Pixel load on selfera.co.uk before the visitor consents, the policy is inaccurate and the site is in breach of PECR.

Note 5: Soft opt-in requirements
The soft opt-in for existing clients only applies if:
The person is an existing customer (i.e. they have entered into a paid relationship)
The marketing is for similar products or services
They were given a clear opt-out option when their details were collected
Every marketing message includes an easy opt-out

If any of these are missing, the soft opt-in does not apply and consent-based marketing is required.

Note 6: Contract preparation basis for consultation bookings
Using contract preparation (Article 6(1)(b)) for consultation bookings is legally strongest because it does not depend on consent (which can be withdrawn). This basis remains valid even if the consultation does not result in a paid engagement. The person requested the consultation, so the pre-contract steps were legitimate.

Note 7: Legal obligation retention periods
The 6-year retention for financial records is set by HMRC and Companies Act. This overrides any request to delete records within the 6-year window. This will be explained in the Retention section (Topic 7).

Note 8: Special category data processing not covered
This topic does not cover special category data (health, biometric, etc.) because Selfera does not process it. If Selfera ever accepts an engagement involving special category data, this topic must be updated to disclose the specific Article 9 basis being relied on. See Topic 2 Note 7 for the escalation process.

Note 1: Automated retention as a mandatory build item
The policy commits to automated retention. This must be built into the dashboard architecture. Practical implementation:
Every table in the database has a retention_expiry_date column
A scheduled job runs daily to identify records past their retention date
Records are either deleted or anonymised depending on the data category
Deletion actions are logged in an immutable audit log for compliance evidence

This is a core dashboard feature. It must be included in Mariselvam's work and Manikandan's documentation.

Note 2: Anonymisation vs deletion
Anonymisation is stronger than pseudonymisation. Once data is truly anonymised, it is no longer personal data and does not fall under UK GDPR. This is the safest approach for:
Financial records after HMRC period expires (aggregate figures kept, individual identifiers stripped)
Product improvement data (usage patterns kept, user identifiers stripped)
Historical analytics (trends kept, individual sessions stripped)

Anonymisation must be irreversible. Simply removing the name field is not enough. Consider:
Removing all direct identifiers
Removing indirect identifiers that could re-identify (IP address, device fingerprint)
Aggregating data so no individual is identifiable

Note 3: Backup handling
The policy commits to backup deletion within 90 days. This must align with actual backup practices:
Supabase backup retention should be set to a maximum of 90 days
Cloudflare R2 versioning if enabled must be reviewed
Any offline backups (external drives, cloud storage) must follow the same 90-day rule
Document the backup schedule and confirm alignment quarterly

Note 4: Legal retention override matrix
Invoices and payment records (HMRC, Finance Act): 6 years from end of accounting period
Tax records (HMRC, Finance Act): 6 years
Payroll records for Selfera India staff (HMRC for UK tax exposure and Indian tax law): 6 years
Statutory company records for Selfera UK Ltd (Companies Act 2006): Life of the company
Contract records (Limitation Act 1980): 6 years
Insurance claim records (Insurance policy requirements): Typically 6 years or as per policy

Note 5: GA4 configuration
Google Analytics 4 defaults to 14 months but is configurable up to 50 months (standard properties) or 26 months (some accounts). Selfera's setting must be checked in the GA4 admin panel and set to 14 months before public launch.

Note 6: Cookie consent records
Under PECR and ICO 2026 guidance, cookie consent records must be kept as evidence of consent for at least 12 months. This proves that consent was obtained when it was given. The consent management platform (cookie banner tool) usually handles this automatically.

Note 7: Retention conflict resolution
Where a data subject's retention rights conflict with legal retention obligations:
1. First, identify what information is legally required
2. Anonymise or pseudonymise the legally required information as much as possible
3. Delete everything else
4. Respond to the data subject explaining what was deleted and what was retained under legal obligation
5. Delete the retained information as soon as the legal period expires
6. Log the retention decision in the compliance folder

Note 8: Retention review annually
The retention schedule must be reviewed annually to check:
Are the periods still appropriate for the business?
Have any new legal obligations arisen?
Are the automated deletion mechanisms working correctly?
Are backups being purged on schedule?

Set an annual review calendar entry.

TOPIC 8 INTERNAL NOTES

Note 1: Identity verification process
For access, deletion, and portability requests, standard identity verification applies. Practical steps:
If the request comes from an email address already on record and matches recent communications, no additional verification needed
If the request comes from an unknown email address or claims to be from an existing contact but from a different address, request one of:
  Photograph of government-issued photo ID (passport, driving licence)
  Video call confirmation
  Response from the email address we already hold on record
Never store copies of ID longer than necessary to verify. Delete immediately after verification.

Document the verification method in the rights request log.

Note 2: Response format flexibility
The policy offers JSON, CSV, and PDF. This flexibility matters because:
Enterprise clients often want JSON for integration purposes
Individuals typically want PDF for readability
Portability requests often want CSV for spreadsheet review

Build a simple export tool in the dashboard that can output all three formats.

Note 3: 30-day response deadline
Missing the 30-day deadline is a compliance breach. Practical steps:
Log every rights request within 24 hours of receipt in the rights request log
Automated deadline reminders at day 15 and day 25
If extension to 60 days needed, notify the requester in writing before day 30 with clear reasons
If refusing, notify before day 30 with reasons and ICO complaint route

Note 4: Rights request log (internal, not published)
Maintain an internal log of all rights requests including:
Date received
Requester name and email
Type of request
Verification method used
Date of response
Actions taken
Any deletion or restriction applied
Copy of the response sent

This log is evidence of compliance and must be retained for at least 3 years.

Note 5: AI-related rights disclosure
The special note on AI is proactive and forward-looking. When responding to AI-related rights requests:
Be able to identify which AI systems processed which data
Be able to describe the purpose and any outputs
Be honest about limitations (some AI processes may not be fully reconstructable)
Never refuse the question. Always explain what we know and do not know.

Note 6: Suppression list handling
When someone unsubscribes from marketing or objects to direct marketing:
Delete all marketing profile data immediately
Keep only the email address on a suppression list
The suppression list must be checked before every marketing send
The suppression list is never used for any other purpose
The suppression list itself must be secured with restricted access

Note 7: Refusal grounds
Refusing a rights request is high risk. Only refuse if:
The request is clearly vexatious or excessive under the DUAA standard
Legal or contractual obligations require retention (partial refusal only)
Identity cannot be verified after reasonable attempts

Clear reason
Legal basis for the refusal
Right to complain to the ICO
Confirmation that we can revisit if additional information is provided

Log every refusal in the compliance folder with the analysis.

Note 8: Portability format standardisation
Data portability requires machine-readable format. Standard practice:
JSON with well-documented schema
CSV with headers matching database field names
Include a README file explaining the structure
Deliver via secure link (Cloudflare R2 with expiring URL) rather than email attachment for large files

Note 9: Third party requests
When a solicitor, family member, or agent makes a request on behalf of another person:
Request written authorisation (letter of authority, power of attorney)
Verify the identity of both the requester and the person they represent
Only disclose information covered by the authorisation
If in doubt, contact the data subject directly to confirm

Requires clarity on what personal data was used to generate AI outputs
Requires clarity on whether outputs are stored or discarded

Build simple logging into any AI integration so this can be answered.

TOPIC 9 INTERNAL NOTES

Note 1: Custom cookie consent system architecture
Selfera will build its own cookie consent management system instead of using a third-party tool. This is a strategic build because the same capability will be reused across client dashboards later. Development effort is 2 to 4 weeks.

Note 2: Database structure for consent records
Three tables required:
cookie_consent_records: one row per consent action per visitor
cookie_policy_versions: versioned policy for audit trail
cookie_registry: the master list of all cookies with categories, purposes, and durations

Note 3: Consent record retention
Consent records are kept for 12 months from the date of consent. This aligns with the annual re-consent cycle. After 12 months, records can be deleted unless needed for an ongoing legal matter.

Note 4: Script blocking is a non-negotiable technical requirement
GA4, Meta Pixel, LinkedIn Insight Tag, and any other non-essential scripts MUST NOT load before consent is given. The technical implementation must:
Load only the cookie banner and strictly necessary scripts on first render
Store consent choice in a first-party cookie called selfera_consent
Conditionally inject third-party scripts based on stored preferences
Never rely on the third-party script's own consent mechanism

If this is not built correctly, the site is in breach of PECR regardless of the banner appearance.

Note 5: Cookie registry maintenance
The cookie registry table is the single source of truth for what cookies are in use. Every new integration must add its cookies to this table before going live. This registry drives the public-facing cookie list on the Cookie Policy page.

Note 6: Cookie policy versioning
Every material change to the Cookie Policy creates a new version. Old versions must be archived and accessible for compliance purposes. Consent records reference the version that was live when consent was given.

Note 7: Consent banner design requirements
Under the April 2026 ICO guidance, the banner must include:
"Accept All" button
"Reject All" button (equal prominence to Accept)
"Customise" or "Manage preferences" option
No pre-ticked boxes
No forced consent to access the site
No cookie walls
Clear, plain-language description of each category

Test the banner against the ICO's example non-compliant patterns before launch.

Note 8: Twelve-month re-consent
The 12-month consent expiry must be enforced automatically. When a visitor returns after 12 months, the banner reappears and their previous consent is not honoured until they re-confirm.

Note 9: Cookie preferences link
A "Cookie preferences" link must appear in the footer of every page. Clicking it must reopen the same consent screen. This is a legal requirement, not a nice-to-have.

Note 10: IP anonymisation for GA4
GA4 anonymises IP addresses by default for European visitors, but this should be explicitly confirmed in the GA4 admin panel and documented in the compliance folder.

Note 11: Server-side tracking option kept open
The policy states we do not use server-side tracking currently. If Selfera adopts server-side tracking later (Google Analytics server-side, Meta Conversions API server-side), this section must be updated and re-published before implementation.

Note 12: Third-party cookie phase-out contingency plan
When Google fully deprecates third-party cookies in Chrome (expected 2027), several tracking mechanisms will break. Selfera must monitor this and update tracking approach when it happens. Add annual review of tracking landscape to the compliance calendar.
